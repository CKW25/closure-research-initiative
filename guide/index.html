Where to Begin

The seven papers and the monograph develop a single argument, but they approach it from different angles and at different levels of formality. Some introduce the foundational mathematical machinery; others apply it to specific problems or unify results across domains. This guide suggests an order of reading that builds the picture systematically, with a brief alternative path for those with particular interests.

Each entry below links to its dedicated page, where the abstract, BibTeX citation, and download links are collected.

Primary Sequence

  1. Overview
    A non-technical introduction to the program’s central question and results. Begin here to understand the overall shape before engaging the formal work.
  2. Closed Comparison Worlds
    The mathematical starting point. Develops the theory of binary comparison data, rectangular completeness, profile-maximality, and the obstruction to subsystem attribution in a finitary setting. This paper establishes the foundational architecture on which the rest of the program rests.
  3. Closed System Monograph
    The comprehensive treatise. Builds the full theory from comparison data through quotient semantics, transport obstruction, and the emergence of smooth geometry, culminating in the S³ theorem. Read this after CCW for the complete argument.
  4. Route Invariants and the Factorization Criterion
    A self-contained result with wide applicability. The factorization criterion records the same structural obstruction found in quotient semantics, gauge theory, and general relativity. This paper can be read independently but gains force in the context of the monograph.
  5. Four Axes of Structural Error
    Applies the closure framework to diagnose foundational problems across physics. The four axes—externalization, artificial factorization, premature globalization, reification of repair—are proved independent and shown to recur across theories. A taxonomic supplement to the constructive work.
  6. Structural Closure Conditions
    Examines the closure conditions—rectangular completeness and profile-maximality—in broader structural contexts. Extends the analysis of CCW to settings where the comparison structure carries additional algebraic or geometric data.
  7. Comparison Foliations and Smooth Geometry
    Addresses the transition from discrete or finitary comparison data to a smooth geometric arena. The foliation structure of the comparison groupoid is shown to force smoothness under mild conditions.
  8. Route Invariants and the Geometry of Gauge Transport
    Applies the factorization criterion to gauge transport and holonomy, demonstrating that the obstruction to endpoint-determinedness in gauge theories is an instance of the same structural pattern identified across the program.

Alternative Paths

For the mathematically inclined reader. Begin with CCW for the finitary foundations, then proceed through CSM for the full construction. The remaining papers — RC, FE, SCC, CFSG, RIE — may be read in any order after the monograph, as each addresses a specific aspect of the program.

For the physicist interested in structural foundations. Begin with the Overview, then read FE and RC, which connect the closure framework to familiar problems in gauge theory and general relativity. The monograph CSM may be consulted for the technical development of the geometry.

For the reader interested in the S³ theorem alone. The Overview states the result in plain language. The full demonstration appears in CSM (Sections 5–7), with the necessary preliminaries developed in CCW and CFSG.
