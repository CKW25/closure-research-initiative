Logical Status

This page states the current logical status of several central commitments in the closure program. The distinctions below are structural: theorem-level results proved under stated hypotheses, criterion-level commitments that define the program’s notion of closedness, and interface conditions required when the transport tower is read as smooth geometry.

1. Rectangular completeness

Status. The primitive at the comparison-world level is intrinsicality: a comparison is a relation among states, invariant under the automorphisms of the comparison world it helps constitute, rather than a label imposed from outside. Under the hypotheses stated in CSM Theorem 2.6.4 — especially local distinguishability and self-containment — rectangular completeness follows from that primitive. The finite-to-global boundary is handled separately by the refinement-tower and finite-witness results.

Scope. Rectangular completeness is not asserted for every physical subsystem. Rods, heat baths, and symmetric comparison worlds remain genuine open systems in this terminology. The theorem says that a self-contained, locally distinguishable comparison world contains no admissible profile-pair omission invisible to its own comparison structure. Systems that fail this condition are classified as open because their constraints are not generated by the internal comparison data alone.

2. The closure criterion

Status. The substantive criterion is the identification of closed comparison worlds with worlds having no intrinsically undetectable profile-pair omissions. CSM Lemma 2.6.2 shows that an admissible but unrealized profile pair can be adjoined conservatively: its addition need not disturb the existing finite comparison algebra. CSM Corollary 2.6.3 then identifies closure as the exclusion of such conservative omissions.

On this reading, a missing profile pair that is internally admissible but unrealized is not a harmless absence. It is a selection not accounted for by the comparison data. A closed world is therefore one in which admissible combinations are either realized or internally excluded. The rod example and the deleted-cell example are useful precisely because they separate closed systems from constrained open ones.

The standard two-subsystem comparison in RC checks this criterion against familiar physics: in the classical case it recovers the full-product closed component, and in the quantum case it captures the product-state sector. The criterion is therefore stated as a formal admissibility standard, not as ordinary-language usage of the word “closed.”

3. Relation to structural realism

The program shares a broad diagnosis with ontic structural realism [1–3]: physical content should be invariant relational structure rather than externally attached labels. Its additional claim is formal. Intrinsicality is used as a generative constraint from which rectangular completeness, quotient descent, transport obstruction, and geometric rigidity are developed under stated hypotheses.

Structural realism supplies philosophical orientation; the closure program supplies a theorem-driven comparison architecture. The point of contact is the relational premise. The difference is that the monograph treats the premise as a formal admissibility condition and follows its consequences through comparison worlds, quotient semantics, transport, curvature, and the closed three-dimensional realized case.

4. Faithful smooth realization and the ghost-free interface

Status. The geometric branch reads the stabilized degree-2 transport carrier as smooth curvature. The current interface is explicit: condition (T) controls torsion in the stabilized quadratic carrier, axiom (D) supplies uniform defect detectability on the tower, and under (T)+(D) the Malcev completion of the two-step transport quotient provides a canonical realization whose second-jet comparison map is injective by construction.

The ghost counter-models identify the exact failure modes. Torsion classes are invisible to every real-linear reading, and profinite completions admit no differential reading. The structure trichotomy (via Gleason–Yamabe and van Dantzig) shows that every scale-compatible completion of the transport law is Lie, totally disconnected, or mixed; axiom (D) selects the Lie branch. Condition (T) is a theorem in the Magnus regime, where stage carriers embed in the exterior square of the degree-1 lattice, with the cited Magnus, Witt, Labute, and Duchamp–Krob inputs.

The second-jet layer is selected by the intrinsic filtration, not by an arbitrary preference among first-, second-, and third-order smooth data. First-order data records the transport or connection level, but it does not yet contain the closed-loop tensorial obstruction. The first stable obstruction is the quadratic square-defect carrier F2/F3; under smooth realization this is the infinitesimal-holonomy layer. Standard differential geometry supports the smooth side of that reading: curvature is detected by infinitesimal holonomy around small two-cells, and in normal coordinates first-derivative metric data can be removed while curvature is carried by second-order data [4–6]. Higher jets refine the later tower after curvature is already visible.

Logical summary. Rectangular completeness is a theorem under the comparison-world hypotheses stated in the monograph. The closure criterion is the program’s explicit admissibility standard. The faithful-realization interface is the ghost-free regime (T)+(D), with canonical realization under that pair. The S³ theorem is the manifold-stage rigidity result obtained in that setting.
