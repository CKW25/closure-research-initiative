Logical Status

This page classifies the main claims by logical role. It distinguishes primitive definitions, theorem-level results under stated hypotheses, criterion-level commitments, interface conditions, and conditional physical consequences. The binding statements are the cited definitions, hypotheses, and theorems in the monograph and supporting papers.

1. Primitive level

Status: primitive/admissibility condition. The primitive object is a comparison world (U,C): states together with comparison predicates. The primitive admissibility condition is intrinsicality: a comparison is a relation among states, invariant under the automorphisms of the comparison world it helps constitute, rather than a label imposed from outside. This is the starting condition of the program, not a theorem inside the program.

2. Rectangular completeness

Status: theorem under stated comparison-world hypotheses. Under the hypotheses stated in CSM Theorem 2.6.4 — especially intrinsicality, local distinguishability, and the self-containment condition expressed by the conservative-completion argument — rectangular completeness follows. The finite-to-global boundary is handled separately by the refinement-tower and finite-witness results.

Scope. Rectangular completeness is not asserted for every physical subsystem. Rods, heat baths, and symmetric comparison worlds remain genuine open systems in this terminology. The theorem says that a self-contained, locally distinguishable comparison world contains no admissible profile-pair omission invisible to its own comparison structure. Systems that fail this condition are classified as open because their constraints are not generated by the internal comparison data alone.

3. Closure criterion

Status: definition/criterion. The program uses “closed” at the comparison stage for worlds with no admissible profile-pair omission: admissible combinations are either realized or internally excluded. CSM Lemma 2.6.2 shows that an admissible but unrealized profile pair can be adjoined conservatively, without disturbing the existing finite comparison algebra; CSM Corollary 2.6.3 identifies closure as the exclusion of such conservative omissions.

The standard two-subsystem comparison in RC checks this criterion against familiar physics: in the classical case it recovers the full-product closed component, and in the quantum case it captures the product-state sector. The criterion is a formal admissibility standard, not ordinary-language usage of the word “closed.”

4. Quotient semantics and subsystem attribution

Status: theorem-level quotient consequences. Once the relevant group action and quotient are fixed, admissible reports factor through the orbit quotient. In the two-subsystem setting, pure orbit loops can have twin representatives with different subsystem attributions but the same quotient-level report values. Thus subsystem attribution is not invariantly recoverable from quotient content alone. The finitary version is developed in CCW.

5. Faithful smooth realization

Status: interface condition with exact decomposition. The geometric branch reads the stabilized degree-2 transport carrier as smooth curvature. The current interface is explicit: condition (T) controls torsion in the stabilized quadratic carrier, axiom (D) supplies uniform defect detectability on the tower, and under (T)+(D) the Malcev completion of the two-step transport quotient provides a canonical realization whose second-jet comparison map is injective by construction.

The ghost counter-models identify the exact failure modes. Torsion classes are invisible to every real-linear reading, and profinite completions admit no differential reading. The structure trichotomy (via Gleason–Yamabe and van Dantzig) shows that every scale-compatible completion of the transport law is Lie, totally disconnected, or mixed; axiom (D) selects the Lie branch. Condition (T) is a theorem in the Magnus regime, where stage carriers embed in the exterior square of the degree-1 lattice, with the cited Magnus, Witt, Labute, and Duchamp–Krob inputs.

The second-jet layer is selected by the intrinsic filtration, not by an arbitrary preference among first-, second-, and third-order smooth data. First-order data records the transport or connection level, but it does not yet contain the closed-loop tensorial obstruction. The first stable obstruction is the quadratic square-defect carrier F2/F3; under smooth realization this is the infinitesimal-holonomy layer. Standard differential geometry supports the smooth side of that reading: curvature is detected by infinitesimal holonomy around small two-cells, and in normal coordinates first-derivative metric data can be removed while curvature is carried by second-order data [1–3]. Higher jets refine the later tower after curvature is already visible.

6. S³ rigidity

Status: manifold-stage rigidity theorem. The S³ result is the CFSG theorem at the faithful smooth-realization stage. Under the stated closed-system and smooth-realization hypotheses, admissible comparison morphisms force frame-bundle transitivity; the standard homogeneous-geometry result then forces constant sectional curvature, and the simply connected compact space-form case is S³. The compactification picture on Foundations is orientation, not the proof.

7. Physical consequences

Status: conditional recovery and prediction claims. Standard frameworks enter as recovery targets. Their structures are classified according to whether they are recovered from the closure architecture or retained as explicit interface conditions. The public empirical consequence is likewise conditional: if the closed electromagnetic phase sector constructed in CSM Chapter 18 is physically realized and the primitive charge unit is normalized by the observed electron charge magnitude, then free/asymptotic vacuum electromagnetic charges lie in the denominator-3 lattice.

Internal source trail: intrinsicality, rectangular completeness, finite-to-global results, transport obstruction, and the faithful-realization interface are in CSM; the finitary comparison-world version is in CCW; the standard two-subsystem comparison is in RC; the S³ rigidity theorem is isolated in CFSG; the charge-sector consequence is stated on Predictions. References [1]–[3] support the standard differential-geometric background behind the smooth reading of curvature and second-order data; they are not sources for the closure program’s own theorem claims.
