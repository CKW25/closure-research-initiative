This monograph is written as a theorem-first study of closed relational
systems.  Its claims are organized by logical status.  Definitions fix the
primitive objects, intrinsic theorems state what follows from those objects
and their admissibility conditions, realization-level statements record what
follows after a smooth, Hilbert, phase, observer-side, or quantitative
reading has been introduced, and boundary data are kept explicit when they
are not derived inside the intrinsic stack.

The purpose of this convention is to prevent background structure from
entering unnoticed.  A manifold, field bundle, Hilbert space, observer
frame, measurement scale, or global parameter may be used only after its
status has been fixed: either it is reconstructed from the comparison
system, or it is named as realization data for a later reading.  The monograph
therefore treats familiar geometric and physical structures neither as
primitive arenas nor as discarded artifacts, but as objects whose
admissibility must be accounted for.

The binding claims of the text are the definitions, theorem statements,
proofs, and hypotheses attached to them.  Expository summaries are included
to make the dependency chain readable; they do not replace the formal
statements.  When a result depends on smooth realization, ghost exclusion,
scale coherence, observer-side readout, or quantitative boundary data, that
dependence is part of the result.

The Reader Guide gives practical routes through the monograph.  The
Introduction begins the mathematical argument: it states the primitive
comparison object, the closed-world admissibility principle, and the main
structural theorem whose proof is distributed across the chapters.
