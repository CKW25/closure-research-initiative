How the program was found

The closure program was not begun from its premise. It was begun from a question, in 2009: which of the structures that physics treats as given are genuinely necessary, and which are imported only by habit? There was no intention, at the start, of arriving at a single principle. The intention was to look.

The method was slow and concrete. Begin at the foundations of physics and rebuild the classical theories one at a time—mechanics, then the field theories, then the relativistic and quantum structures—each reconstructed from the ground rather than inherited. The point of rebuilding a theory you already know is that you find out which of its parts you were assuming without warrant. Done one theory at a time, over years, a pattern began to surface that none of the individual reconstructions had been looking for: the same kind of imported structure kept appearing in each, in different costume—an external frame here, a pre-supplied background there, a vantage standing outside the system and doing work the system could not do for itself.

The thread underneath all of them was closure. Each theory failed to be self-contained in the same way, and each repair pointed at the same missing requirement: that the system’s structure be recoverable from within, with nothing outside it doing the defining. Closure was not a starting assumption that the program set out to defend. It was what the reconstructions kept demanding, the common cause behind problems that had looked unrelated. The single premise the work now rests on was the last thing found, not the first—the destination the long route of rebuilding arrived at.

The premise came last, and that reorganized everything

For most of the program’s history the foundation was not a single premise but several apparently separate commitments—that the systems in question are closed, that their finite structure fixes their global structure, that comparison is the right primitive. These were carried as independent assumptions for years, because they looked independent.

They are not. The recognition that all of them descend from one requirement—that a comparison be intrinsic, a genuine relation among states rather than a labeling imposed from outside—came late, and when it came it reorganized everything before it. What had been a list of standing principles became a single premise with the rest as consequences: closure and the finite-to-global boundary stopped being assumptions and became theorems. The work now presents the result in its finished logical order, premise first. That order is very nearly the reverse of the order in which it was found.

Structural closure is not cosmological closure

One distinction took years to state cleanly, and it is worth isolating because the words invite confusion. The mathematical sense of closure in this program—closure under intrinsic comparison—is not the cosmological sense, the open-versus-closed universe of FLRW cosmology. They are different ideas at different logical levels. The theorem that a genuinely closed three-dimensional system is the three-sphere is a result about structural closure; it is not a claim about whether the cosmos is spatially closed.

The two senses were entangled in the program’s own early thinking, and disentangling them was not quick. Structural Closure and the Cosmological Misnomer develops the separation in full.

What is at the bottom

The long route of rebuilding kept pushing the foundation downward. Each thing that looked like the floor turned out to rest on something further down. Closure looked fundamental, and then closure was derived. The requirement that finite structure fix global structure looked like a separate assumption, and then it too was derived. Each candidate for the bottom had something beneath it.

What the regress finally reached was not a deeper object but a condition—and the mark of its being the bottom is that nothing lies beneath it, because nothing can. A comparison is a relation, not a labeling. To be a distinction is to be a symmetry-invariant of comparison: there is no fact telling two states apart beyond how they stand to one another under the world’s own symmetries. This cannot be derived from anything more basic, because anything more basic would have to be a fact about states grounded in nothing relational—and a fact grounded in nothing relational is precisely the external labeling the requirement forbids. The floor is self-justifying in the only way a true floor can be: to ask what grounds it is already to have stepped outside the system, which a self-contained system does not permit. The question “what is beneath this?” stops being askable from the inside.

So there is no bottom object. There is the recognition that there was never an outside to stand on—and that this, stated exactly, is enough to force everything above it: the rigidity, the curvature, the geometry of the three-sphere, the rest. The structure of physics, on this account, is what self-containment cannot avoid becoming, and self-containment bottoms out not in a thing but in the absence of any vantage other than the world’s own.

The seven preprints and the monograph are public. They present the finished theory; these notes record how it was arrived at.
